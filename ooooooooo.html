INTRODUCTION

  • Inflation & Monetary Policy – Milton Friedman stated that inflation is always a monetary phenomenon, and McCallum (1989) emphasised the role of monetary institutions in economic stability.

  • Price Stability & Growth – Monetary policy aims to control inflation and support sustainable economic growth through tools like interest rates and open market operations.

  • The SBP’s monetary policy aims to achieve the goal of price stability without neglecting the objective of economic growth.

  • Monetary Policy Transmission Mechanism: refers to the process through which changes in SBP’s policy rate influence inflation, output, and economic activity. It works through interest rates, exchange rates, asset prices, and expectations channels to stabilize inflation.

Objectives of the Study

• To check the impact of SBP Policy Rate on key macroeconomic indicators including: inflation, exchange rates, foreign reserves and money supply.

• To assess the structural shocks within the Bayesian SVAR model, through Bayesian estimation and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) simulation methods incorporating SGT-distributed errors, to analyze how monetary policy affects the broader economy, especially under non-Gaussian error assumptions.

Research Gap

  • Traditional VAR models with Gaussian errors fail to capture nonlinear and asymmetric monetary policy effects.
  • BSVAR with skewed generalized t-distributions (SGT) improves accuracy by addressing non-Gaussian errors .
  • Enhanced Monetary Policy Analysis for Pakistan

This study applies the BSVAR model (Anttonen et al., 2024) with Bayesian estimation and MCMC simulations, addressing inconsistencies in prior research by improving the identification of structural shocks and providing more reliable insights into SBP’s monetary policy transmission and its macroeconomic impacts.

Research Question

• What are the impact of SBP Policy Rate on key macroeconomic indicators including inflation, exchange rates, reserves money, foreign reserves and money supply?

Significance of the Study

  • Uses the Bayesian Structural Vector Autoregression (BSVAR) model for robust monetary policy analysis.

  • Incorporates skewed generalized t-distributions (SGT) to capture asymmetric and non-linear responses.

  • Evaluates the impact of SBP’s policy rate on CPI, REER, M2, and reserve money.

Econometric Methodology

SVAR Model Specification

The SVAR(p) model is given by:

\[ \begin{equation}y_t = \alpha + A_1 y_{t-1} + ... + A_p y_{t-p} + B \epsilon_t,\end{equation} (1) \]

  • where:

    - \( y_t \) is an \( (n \times 1) \) vector of time series,

    - \( \alpha \) is the intercept,

    - \( A_1, \dots, A_p \) are \( (n \times n) \) coefficient matrices,

    - \( B \) is an \( (n \times n) \) impact matrix with contemporaneous relations of structural errors \( \epsilon_t \), - \( \epsilon_t \) is the structural error term, and \( B \) is assumed to be nonsingular.

Assumptions on Structural Errors introduced by Anttonen et al. (2024)

The structural errors \( \epsilon_t = (\epsilon_{1t},…,\epsilon_{nt})’ \) are assumed nonsingular.

(i) \( \epsilon_t \) is a sequence of serially uncorrelated random vectors with each component \( \epsilon_{it} \), \( i = 1,…,n \), having zero mean and unit scale \( \sigma_i \).

(ii) The components of \( \epsilon_t \) are mutually independent, and at most one of them has a Gaussian marginal distribution.

Identification Problem

1. Statistical Identification via Non-Gaussianity

Structural shocks are assumed to be mutually independent and non-Gaussian (at most one Gaussian), enabling statistical identification of matrix B without imposing zero or sign restrictions (Lanne et al., 2017).

2. B Identified Up to Permutation and Sign

Matrix B is point identified only up to column permutation and sign, resulting in multiple observationally equivalent models.

3. Permutation Issue in Bayesian Estimation

This leads to a multimodal posterior, which is not problematic in Bayesian inference as long as the posterior is proper, but complicates the interpretation of impulse responses

Frequentist Approaches or SVAR model

In frequentist SVARs approaches often impose constraints like zero restrictions or Cholesky decomposition to identify B, which may be too rigid for real-world economic shocks.

5. Bayesian Approaches: Priors & Diagnostics

Bayesian models avoid hard constraints by using:

Nonexchangeables (e.g., shrinking B toward diagonal), and Split- \(\hat{R}\) diagnostics to detect permutation jumps during MCMC sampling.

Skewed Generalized t-Distribution for Errors

Anttonen et al. (2024) propose a more flexible approach by estimating the joint posterior distribution of the parameters in an unrestricted SVAR model.

Structural shocks \(\epsilon_{it}, i = 1, \dots, n\) follow a Skewed Generalized t-Distribution (SGT), allowing for asymmetric and fat-tailed behavior in economic data:

\[ \small f_i (\epsilon_{it}; \lambda_i, p_i, q_i ) = \frac{p_i}{2 \nu_i q_i^{1/p_i}} B\left(\frac{1}{p_i}, q_i\right) \frac{|\epsilon_{it} + m_i|^{p_i}}{q_i^{p_i} \nu_i^{p_i} (\lambda_i \text{sign}(\epsilon_{it} + m_i) + 1)^{p_i}} + 1 \bigg)^{\frac{1}{p_i} + q_i} (2)\]

  • Here ( B() ) denotes the Beta function.
  • The skewness is controlled by \(( \lambda_i )\) with \(( -1 < \lambda_i < 1 )\), where \(( \lambda_i = 0 )\) indicates symmetry.
  • The parameters \(( p_i > 0 )\) and \(( q_i > 0 )\) control the kurtosis, influencing tail behavior.
  • Tail exponent: \(( \alpha_i = p_i q_i)\) Similar to the degrees of freedom in a Student’s ( t )-distribution.
  • When \(( q_i \to \infty )\), the distribution becomes Gaussian.
  • The scale parameter \(( v_i )\) adjusts the spread, and the location parameter \(( m_i )\) shifts the distribution.
  • To ensure the errors \(( \epsilon_{it} )\) conform to assumptions (i) and (ii), we restrict parameters such that:
    • Errors have zero mean.
    • Scale \(( \sigma_i )\) of \(( \epsilon{it} )\) is fixed to unity by setting \(( v_i = 1 )\).
    • Identification relies on:
      • Mutual independence of shocks.
      • Non-Gaussianity (Skitovich–Darmois theorem).
  • We collect parameters controlling skewness and kurtosis into the vector:
    \[ \gamma = (\lambda_1, p_1, q_1, \dots, \lambda_n, p_n, q_n)' \]

Likelihood function and prior distribution

  • Due to the Complexity of equations (1) and (2), Anttonen et al. (2024) estimate parameters using Bayesian methods.
  • This requires: Likelihood Function Construction , Specification of Prior Distributions

Likelihood function:

\[ \begin{equation}p(y|\theta) = |\det(B)|^{-T} \prod_{i=1}^{n} \prod_{t=1}^{T} f_i (\iota_i' B^{-1} u_t(\pi); \lambda_i, p_i, q_i),\end{equation} (3) \]

where:

  • \(\theta = (\pi′,β′,γ′)′\) represents the parameter vector.

  • \(\pi = \text{vec}([α,A′_1:···:A′_p]′)\) includes the VAR coefficients.

  • \(β = \text{vec}(B)\) represents the elements of the structural impact matrix \(B\).

  • \(u_t(\pi) = y_t−α−A_1 y_{t−1}−···−A_p y_{t−p}\) denotes the reduced-form errors.

  • \(ι_i\) is the unit vector selecting the \(i\)-th component of \(B^{-1} u_t(\pi)\)

  • \(\gamma = (\lambda_1, p_1, q_1, \dots, \lambda_n, p_n, q_n)'\):Parameters of SGT distributions.

  • \(fi(⋅)\)​: SGT density function for structural shock \(\epsilon_{it}​.\)

  • Bayesian Estimation (with Simulation):
    SVAR parameters are estimated via simulation using the Differential Evolution Markov Chain with snooker updater (DE-MCz) (Ter Braak & Vrugt, 2008). Convergence is assessed using the Gelman-Rubin statistic \((\hat{R} < 1.10).\)

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Convergence Diagnostics Simulation Analysis

  • Sampling Setup: Two MCMC chains, each with 100,000 draws and 10 inner iterations per step (2 million posterior evaluations total).

  • Burn-in Phase: Discarded up to 820,000 initial samples; final analysis based on 18,000 draws per chain.

  • Convergence Check: Gelman-Rubin statistic \(\hat{R} < 1.10\) for all 492 parameters, confirming stability.

  • Result: Well-mixed chains with no divergences or jumps—ensuring reliable Bayesian inference.

Conclusion

BSVAR Model: The Bayesian Structural Vector Auto Regression (BSVAR) model, utilizing non-Gaussian skewed generalized t-distributed error terms and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) simulation techniques, effectively captured the asymmetry and non-linearity of structural shocks, providing a more accurate representation of economic shocks.

  1. Inflation Control: Aggressive contractionary measures, such as raising the policy rate, are crucial for controlling inflationary shocks.

  2. REER Management: A balanced approach is necessary to manage REER volatility, as initial appreciation can lead to liquidity challenges and decreased long-term competitiveness.

  3. Broad and Reserve Money: Close monitoring of broad money and reserve money is essential to prevent excessive credit buildup and manage inflationary pressures.

Policy Recommendations

  • From Shock 6, interest rate hikes first raised inflation slightly but reduced it over time, showing SBP should clearly explain rate hikes to build trust, so people adjust decisions early and inflation falls faster.

  • From Shock 2, REER appreciation reduced inflation and increased reserves but hurt exports, showing policymakers should support exporters with incentives and manage the exchange rate flexibly to protect jobs and export performance.

  • Monetary policy takes time to show results — SBP must consider this delay.

  1. Inflation (INF) Response:

    • Initially increases by ~30 basis points due to price stickiness and supply-side effects.

    • Gradually declines as contractionary monetary policy reduces demand-side pressures.

    • Expectations and interest rate channels play key roles in stabilizing inflation near zero after 20 period

Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER) Response:

  • Appreciates sharply (~6%) as higher interest rates attract capital inflows.

  • This appreciation negatively affects export competitiveness.

  • Over time, REER returns to baseline, reflecting external account adjustments.

    Broad Money Supply (M2) Response:

  • Initially rises due to liquidity shifts within the banking system.

  • Declines as tighter monetary policy reduces credit availability and money creation.

  • Follows the dynamic: Interest Rate ↑ → Credit Availability ↓ → M2 ↓.

    Reserve Money (RM) Response:

    • Declines sharply due to central bank liquidity tightening measures.

    • The interest rate channel reinforces this decline by discouraging borrowing.

    Total Reserves (TR) Response:

    • Initially increase (~0.5%) due to capital inflows and reduced forex demand.

    • Decline after 10 periods as export competitiveness weakens and external payments rise.

    • Follows the dynamic: Interest Rate ↑ → Capital Inflows ↑ → TR ↑ → Export Competitiveness ↓ → TR ↓.

    Overall Economic Implications:

    • Inflation control through contractionary monetary policy is effective over time.

    • REER appreciation highlights the trade-off between foreign investment and export competitiveness.

    • Broad Money (M2) and Reserve Money (RM) declines confirm the tightening effects of monetary policy.

    • Total Reserves (TR) are vulnerable to prolonged currency appreciation and external imbalances.
